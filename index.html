Liam D. Connor, Ph.D.

I am a Canadian astrophysicist currently living in Los Angeles, working at the California Institute of Technology where I am a Tolman Prize Postdoctoral Fellow. Prior to Caltech, I was a Postdoc at the Anton Pannekoek Institute for Astronomy at the University of Amsterdam. My research is focused on Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs), a mysterious new class of extragalactic explosions. In general I use a variety of statistical, theoretical, and radio astronomical tools to study how the sky changes on very short timescales. I obtained my Ph.D. from the University of Toronto in October 2016, working at the Canadian Institute for Theoretical Astrophysics (CITA) and the Dunlap Institute with Ue-Li Pen and Keith Vanderlinde.

Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs)

Fast Radio Bursts were discovered serendipitously in 2007 with the Parkes telescope in Australia. They are millisecond blasts of radio waves, coming from external galaxies billions of light-years away. Though there appear to be thousands of FRBs across the full sky each day, we still do not know what produces them. However, their tremendous energetics and short duration imply that they must come from extreme environments, whether black holes, the magnetospheres of neutron stars, from the collision of two compact objects, or perhaps something entirely new.

I have been working on this mystery for the last few years. My research includes the proposal of a new theory for the origin of FRBs, developing artificial intelligence algorithms to detect them in real-time on radio telescopes, and applying statistical techniques to the analysis of the FRB population as a whole.

ALERT & Apertif

ALERT will survey the Northern Sky 24/7 at millisecond timescales. It uses two telescopes in tandem, Apertif and LOFAR, both located in the north of the Netherlands. Apertif will detect FRBs at 1.4 GHz and then "trigger" LOFAR's time-machine mode to catch the burst as it arrives a couple of minutes later at 150 MHz. With LOFAR we can localize the FRB to its distant host galaxy. I am currently commissioning ALERT's signal processing backend, including a real-time FRB classifier based on modern artificial intelligence techniques, specifically deep learning. Once we start detecting FRBs we will be able to answer fundamental questions about the nature of this new extragalactic population.

CHIME

During my Ph.D. I helped develop an innovative new radio telescope in British Columbia, Canada. The Canadian Hydrogen Intensity Mapping Experiment (CHIME) consists of four 100-meter long cylinders with no moving parts (parabolic half-pipes, really). CHIME has three exciting and ambitious science goals. The first is to make the largest-ever 3D map of the Universe in order to measure how Dark Energy affects the Universe's dynamics. Another is to study pulsars, monitoring nearly every known pulsar in the northern hemisphere each day. And the third is an experiment to detect large numbers of Fast Radio Bursts, which it began finding in July 2018.
